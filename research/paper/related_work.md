## Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Lewis et al. (2020) introduced RAG as a parametric–nonparametric hybrid: a dense retrieval component fetches supporting documents, and a seq2seq generator conditions on both the query and retrieved content [CITE: Lewis et al., 2020, "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks", NeurIPS].
Subsequent work has scaled the retrieval corpus [CITE: Izacard et al., FiD], improved dense representations [CITE: Karpukhin et al., DPR, 2020], and extended RAG to multi-hop reasoning [CITE: verify].
A recurring finding is that retrieval quality caps answer quality — systems cannot answer faithfully from content they did not retrieve.

Our work extends this: we show that retrieval quality and answer faithfulness are separable, and that a system can retrieve the right chapter while citing the wrong verse within it.

## Faithfulness and Grounding Evaluation

Faithfulness — whether an answer is entailed by its supporting context — is distinct from correctness [CITE: Maynez et al., 2020, "On Faithfulness and Factuality in Abstractive Summarization", ACL].
RAGAS [CITE: Es et al., 2023, "RAGAS: Automated Evaluation of Retrieval Augmented Generation"] decomposes RAG evaluation into faithfulness, answer relevance, and context precision, using an LLM to judge each dimension.
G-Eval [CITE: Liu et al., 2023, "G-Eval: NLG Evaluation using GPT-4 with Better Human Alignment", EMNLP] demonstrates strong correlation between GPT-4 judgments and human ratings for faithfulness-like criteria.
We follow this line by using a Gemini judge to score citation_support, and we add false_premise_refusal as an orthogonal robustness metric.

A known limitation of LLM-as-judge is the same-model bias: a judge tends to score outputs from the same model family higher [CITE: Zheng et al., 2023, "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena", NeurIPS].
Our judge (Gemini) scores answers generated by a different Gemini-family model at answer-generation time; we acknowledge this as a limitation (Section 7) and flag human annotation as necessary future work.

## Bible NLP

Computational engagement with scripture has a history in NLP, primarily through translation alignment [CITE: verify — Bible alignment papers], parallel corpus studies [CITE: verify], and topic modeling over devotional text.
Semantic search over Bible verses has received recent attention in the context of question answering [CITE: verify — any recent Bible QA papers], but citation-faithfulness evaluation has not, to our knowledge, been applied to Bible RAG.

The closest related benchmarks evaluate open-domain religious QA [CITE: verify] rather than verse-grounded attribution.
Our benchmark focuses specifically on the attribution chain: which verse was retrieved, and whether the answer correctly grounds its claims in that verse's text.

## False-Premise Robustness

Robustness to false premises is studied in the broader QA literature [CITE: verify — false premise QA, e.g., Kim et al.].
Bible applications have a specific version of this problem: users may misquote or misattribute verses (e.g., "Spare the rod, spoil the child" is not a direct Bible verse; "God helps those who help themselves" does not appear in scripture).
We include both false-premise and adversarial categories in gold_40 to measure whether the system refuses fabrication requests and corrects misattributions.
